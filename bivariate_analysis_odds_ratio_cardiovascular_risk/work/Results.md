## Results

The bivariate analysis on the *Heart Attack Risk & Prediction Dataset in India* highlights a population of 10,000 individuals, slightly skewed toward males (5,516 vs 4,484 females), with a median age of 49 years and a nearly balanced split between subjects under and over 50 years old.

Among individuals aged over 50, diabetes, obesity, and hypertension emerge as the most relevant factors associated with heart attack risk. In particular, diabetic males over 50 show a heart attack risk rate of 31.77%, followed by obese males (31.14%) and hypertensive males (30.69%). For females in the same age group, family history (30.32%) and healthcare access (29.59%) are among the highest observed proportions.

For individuals under 50, hypertension appears to be the strongest factor among males, with a heart attack risk rate of 31.50%, followed by lack of physical activity (31.07%) and obesity (30.43%). Among females under 50, family history shows the highest proportion (30.62%), followed by air pollution exposure (29.47%) and healthcare access (29.38%).

Overall, the descriptive comparison suggests that men tend to show slightly higher risk percentages than women across several categorical variables, both in the over-50 and under-50 groups. However, these differences are relatively small and do not indicate a single dominant factor.

To further evaluate the strength of association, odds ratios (OR) were calculated for the main categorical variables. The highest OR is observed for low physical activity (1.027), followed by family history (1.006), while all other variables remain close to 1, including hypertension (0.994), obesity (0.991), and diabetes (0.932). This indicates that none of the examined categorical variables shows a strong standalone association with heart attack risk in this dataset.

In summary, the analysis identifies some subgroup-level differences by age and gender, but the odds ratio results suggest that the categorical predictors considered here have only weak individual explanatory power. A multivariate modeling approach would likely be more appropriate to capture the combined effect of these factors on cardiovascular risk.
